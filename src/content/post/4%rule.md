The 4% withdrawal rule is one of the most popular ideas in FIRE. Build a corpus equal to 25 times your annual expenses, withdraw 4% every year adjusted for inflation, and your money should theoretically last for decades. It sounds simple enough to feel universal, but the more I studied where this rule came from, the more I realized it was designed for a very specific environment and not necessarily for India.
## Where the 4% Rule Came From
The rule was introduced by William Bengen and later reinforced through the Trinity Study. It was built around historical U.S. data, relatively lower inflation, mature bond markets, and a 30-year retirement horizon using a diversified stock-bond portfolio. Under those assumptions, it worked reasonably well. But assumptions are everything in retirement planning, and once those assumptions change, the outcome can change too.

I believe India presents a different problem. Inflation can be structurally higher, personal expenses often rise faster than headline inflation suggests, and people pursuing FIRE here may need their corpus to last far beyond 30 years. On top of that, market volatility and sequence-of-return risk make a higher withdrawal rate feel less comfortable. For those reasons, I personally prefer a 3% withdrawal rate and targeting roughly 33 times annual expenses instead of 25 times.

Suppose annual expenses are ₹30 lakh. Under the traditional 4% rule, a corpus of ₹7.5 crore may appear enough. Under a 3% rule, the target becomes ₹10 crore. That additional cushion may seem conservative, but I see it as protection rather than excess. It protects against inflation shocks, poor market decades, and longevity risk.

But for me, the more important insight is not only lowering the withdrawal rate. It is changing how the corpus is managed.
## Why I Don’t Think the 4% Rule Suits India
The biggest reason I question the 4% rule for India is that it was built on assumptions that may not hold here. It emerged from U.S. historical data, lower structural inflation, relatively stable bond markets, and a 30-year retirement horizon. But Indian retirees often face a very different reality.

One major concern is inflation. The 4% rule assumes you can increase withdrawals every year to keep up with inflation, but inflation in India can be far more persistent and unpredictable than what the original studies assumed. Even if broad inflation averages around 5–6%, personal inflation can be much higher. Healthcare, education, domestic services and lifestyle expenses often rise faster than official inflation numbers. Over a 30–40 year retirement, that difference compounds dramatically. A retirement plan that looks comfortable on paper today can become strained decades later simply because expenses rise faster than expected.

Another issue is market volatility. The original rule was built around U.S. markets and a strong bond component that historically acted as a stabilizer. Indian markets can be far more volatile, and our debt instruments do not always play the same protective role U.S. Treasuries historically did. That matters because retirement is not only about long-term average returns — it is about what happens when returns arrive in the wrong order.

This brings in one of the biggest risks in retirement planning: sequence-of-return risk. Imagine retiring just before a major market correction. If your portfolio falls sharply in the first few years while you continue withdrawing 4%, you may be forced to sell assets at depressed prices. That can permanently damage the corpus, even if markets recover later. A withdrawal rate that may look sustainable under average conditions can become risky when bad returns arrive early.
Suppose someone retires with ₹7.5 crore, following the traditional 4% rule, and withdraws ₹30 lakh annually.

Now imagine a 30% market correction happens in the first two years of retirement.

The portfolio may fall close to ₹5 crore, while withdrawals continue.

Now the same ₹30 lakh withdrawal is no longer effectively 4%.

It has become roughly 6% of the reduced corpus.

That changes the math completely.

Even if markets recover later, early withdrawals during a downturn can cause permanent damage to sustainability.

This is why I think the 4% rule can look safe in average conditions, but become fragile under poor early return sequences.

Longevity risk is another reason I find the 4% rule aggressive for India, especially for FIRE. The original rule was designed around roughly a 30-year retirement. But many people pursuing financial independence may retire in their 40s and need their money to last 45 or even 50 years. Extending a strategy designed for 30 years into a 50-year horizon without adjustment can be dangerous.

Taxes also add pressure that many simple FIRE calculators ignore. Withdrawals may not be as frictionless as theory assumes. Capital gains taxes, taxation on debt instruments, and changing tax rules can all create drag over time. Even a small drag matters when compounded over decades.

When you combine higher inflation, more volatile markets, sequence risk, longer retirement periods and tax drag, a 4% withdrawal rate starts looking less like a “safe rule” and more like an optimistic assumption.

That is why I personally prefer a 3% withdrawal rate. Not because 4% always fails, but because 3% gives far more room for uncertainty — and in retirement planning, uncertainty is exactly what needs to be respected.

## Why I Prefer the 3% Rule

After understanding the risks around the 4% rule, I started looking at what would feel more robust for Indian conditions. That is where I found myself leaning toward a 3% withdrawal rate. It may sound like a small difference from 4%, but in retirement planning a 1% difference can be enormous because it affects both the size of the corpus and the probability of the corpus surviving long periods.

The logic is simple. If you plan to withdraw only 3% of your corpus every year, the corpus required becomes roughly 33 times your annual expenses. That becomes my preferred FIRE formula.

**FIRE Corpus = Annual Expenses × 33**

This is very different from the traditional 25x rule that comes from the 4% framework.

Suppose annual expenses in retirement are ₹30 lakh.

Using the traditional 4% rule:

₹30 lakh × 25 = ₹7.5 crore

Using a 3% withdrawal rule:

₹30 lakh × 33 = ₹9.9 crore

Or roughly ₹10 crore.

That extra corpus may seem large, but I do not see it as over-saving. I see it as building resilience. It creates room for inflation surprises, poor return sequences, taxes, healthcare shocks and longer retirements.

For me, that margin of safety is worth far more than trying to optimize for the smallest retirement number.

## Why 33 Times Expenses Makes Sense to Me

The reason I like thinking in terms of 33 times expenses is that it shifts FIRE away from “minimum viable retirement” toward “durable retirement.”

A 25x target may work if many assumptions go right.

A 33x target assumes some things may go wrong.

And I prefer planning with that mindset.

If expenses rise faster than expected, the buffer helps.

If markets underperform for a decade, the buffer helps.

If retirement lasts forty or fifty years, the buffer helps.

In many ways, 33x is not about chasing a bigger number.

It is about buying peace of mind.

## The Bucket Strategy I Prefer

Rather than viewing retirement money as one large portfolio from which withdrawals happen continuously, I prefer dividing the corpus into separate buckets based on time horizon. Each bucket serves a purpose, and together they create a system that balances liquidity, stability and long-term growth.

### Bucket 1 — Safety Bucket (0–3 Years)

- Keep around **three years of expenses** here.  
- For ₹30 lakh annual expenses, that means **₹90 lakh**.  
- Park this money in **liquid funds**.  
- Expected returns may be around **6%**.  
- This is the bucket used for withdrawals.  
- Because it still earns modest returns, it may partly replenish itself.

This bucket ensures that near-term spending is insulated from market volatility.

### Bucket 2 — Stability Bucket (3–6 Years)

- Keep another **₹90 lakh** here.  
- Use **arbitrage or short-duration debt funds**.  
- This acts as the backup bucket.  
- Its role is to refill Bucket 1 when needed.  
- Focus here is capital preservation, not aggressive returns.

This creates a second layer of protection before touching growth assets.

### Bucket 3 — Growth Cushion (7–10 Years)

- Allocate around **₹1.2 crore** here.  
- Use **balanced advantage funds** or hybrid strategies.  
- Expected returns may be around **9%**.  
- This bucket supports Bucket 2 over time.  
- It provides moderate growth while reducing volatility relative to pure equity.

This bucket acts as a bridge between safety and long-term growth.

### Bucket 4 — Long-Term Growth Bucket (10+ Years)

- Remaining corpus, roughly **₹7 crore**, sits here.  
- Invest in long-term growth assets such as diversified equity or index funds.  
- This is the compounding engine of the strategy.  
- Since this money may remain untouched for years, it gets time to grow.

This is what gives the portfolio long-term sustainability.

## How Rebalancing Works

The strength of the strategy lies in how the buckets support each other.

- Withdrawals happen only from **Bucket 1**.  
- When Bucket 1 runs lower, **Bucket 2 replenishes Bucket 1**.  
- Over time, **Bucket 3 replenishes Bucket 2**.  
- During favorable markets, **Bucket 4 replenishes Bucket 3**.

Think of it as cascading reservoirs.

Growth supports moderate growth.

Moderate growth supports stability.

Stability supports withdrawals.

Because of this structure, growth assets ideally need not be sold during poor markets simply to generate income. That may significantly reduce sequence-of-return risk.

## What Could Happen Over 30–40 Years

Suppose the ₹7 crore growth bucket compounds around 11% over long periods. In some years, that could generate growth far exceeding annual withdrawals of ₹30 lakh. While no outcome is guaranteed, this creates a possibility that the corpus may preserve its real value or potentially continue growing even while funding retirement.

Over 30 years, the portfolio may still retain substantial value if withdrawals remain disciplined and rebalancing is done thoughtfully. Over 40 years, the lower withdrawal rate combined with bucket protection gives the corpus a much better chance of surviving compared with a more aggressive drawdown strategy.

That is why I think of this less as simply withdrawing from retirement savings and more as managing a potentially self-sustaining corpus.

## Final Thoughts

I do not believe the 4% rule is broken. I simply believe it belongs to a different context. For India, I find a 3% withdrawal rate combined with a disciplined bucket strategy much more compelling because it prioritizes resilience over optimism.

For me, FIRE is not just about reaching a number.

It is about building a system that helps that number survive.

And for that, I trust structure more than formulas.

This is my personal framework and thought process, not financial advice. Returns, inflation, taxes and risk assumptions vary and should always be evaluated for your own circumstances.
