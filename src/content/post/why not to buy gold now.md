## PM Modi Said Something Most People Ignored

Let's start with what actually happened.

Amid rising global tensions — wars, sanctions, dollar volatility, central bank chaos — PM Narendra Modi made a statement that flew under the radar for most retail investors.

He said:

> *"Gold purchases are another area where foreign exchange is used extensively. In the national interest, we must resolve not to purchase gold for a year."*

Most people saw the headline, scrolled past it, and then opened a jewellery store app to check today's gold rates.

Classic.

But before you dismiss it as just another political soundbite, let's understand what he was actually saying — and more importantly, what it means for *your* money.

---

## Why Gold Imports Are a National Problem

India has a complicated relationship with gold.

We are one of the largest gold consumers in the world. Every year, India imports hundreds of tonnes of gold — for weddings, festivals, investments, gifts, and the general feeling that life is incomplete without a gold chain.

Here's the problem:

Gold doesn't grow in Indian soil. We import almost all of it.

And gold is priced in **US dollars**.

So when Indians buy gold, India needs to spend **foreign exchange** — primarily dollars — to pay for those imports. This increases the **current account deficit**, which is essentially how much more India is spending abroad versus earning.

Now, during a global crisis, a war, or a period of extreme uncertainty:

- Dollar demand spikes globally
- Rupee weakens against the dollar
- India's import bill for *everything* — oil, electronics, machinery — goes up
- Adding gold imports on top of that puts extra pressure on forex reserves

PM Modi's request was essentially:

*"Please don't make a difficult situation worse by importing something non-essential with dollars we don't have to spare right now."*

Patriotic? Yes.

But here's what most people don't realize:

**When Indians buy gold during global panic, they often get hit twice.**

## The Double Impact Nobody Talks About

Let me show you this with simple math.

**Normal times:**

| Factor | Value |
|---|---|
| Global gold price | $2,000 per ounce |
| USD/INR rate | ₹75 |
| Indian equivalent | ₹1,50,000 |

Makes sense. You buy gold at ₹1.5 lakh per ounce (roughly).

Now watch what happens during a global crisis:

**Crisis scenario:**

| Factor | Value |
|---|---|
| Global gold price | $2,400 per ounce (+20%) |
| USD/INR rate | ₹90 (+20%) |
| Indian equivalent | ₹2,16,000 |

That's a **44% increase** in rupee terms — even though gold only rose 20% in dollar terms.

And we haven't even added:

- **Import duty** (currently around 15%)
- **GST** (3% on gold)
- **Local dealer premiums**
- **Making charges** (if you're buying jewellery)

By the time gold reaches your wrist or your portfolio, you've paid a significant premium over the international price.

You're not just buying expensive gold.

**You're buying expensive dollars wrapped inside expensive gold.**

This is the hidden tax Indians pay for buying gold during a crisis that nobody on your family WhatsApp group will ever mention.

What do you notice?

Gold doesn't go up every year.

There are long, frustrating stretches where gold goes nowhere. From 2013 to 2018, gold prices in India were largely flat or slightly negative in real terms. If you had bought gold at the peak of the 2012 rally — after all the headlines — you would have waited nearly 6 years just to break even.

Then suddenly, it rallies 40–60% in a short window.

This is the pattern: **years of silence, followed by sudden explosive moves.**

The problem is — most retail investors buy during the explosive phase and sell (or panic) during the silence.

**Gold is a long-term store of value.**

It is not a "buy now because it's going up" trade.

## Before Crisis vs After Crisis: The Rally Pattern

Here's another data point worth understanding.

Gold's biggest annual returns happen *during* and *immediately after* major global crises.

| Crisis Period | Gold's Behaviour |
|---|---|
| 2008 Financial Crisis | Huge rally as markets collapsed |
| 2012–2018 | Largely flat or negative real returns |
| COVID-19 (2020) | Massive rally to all-time highs |
| 2021–2022 | Flat, then choppy |
| Russia-Ukraine War (2022) | Sharp spike, then partial correction |
| 2023–2025 | Multi-year rally driven by central bank buying |

![Gold Crisis Rally Chart](/assets/images/posts/gold-crisis.png)

See the pattern?

Gold rallies hard during panic. Then it often consolidates or goes flat for a while. Then panic strikes again and it rallies again.

The investors who made money in gold were the ones who bought it **quietly, during the flat periods, when nobody cared.**

Not during the panic.

Not after the 50% rally.

Not when your jeweller put a new "GOLD INVESTMENT SCHEME" banner outside his shop.

## Why We Love Buying Gold At Peak Prices

This is the part that nobody talks about — but everyone needs to hear.

**Investor Psychology: The Gold Edition**

When gold was doing nothing for 4 years:

- No one was interested
- It wasn't a "hot topic" at dinner
- Financial influencers weren't making content about it
- Your relatives weren't giving you investment advice about it

Then gold rises 50–60% in 18 months:

- Suddenly everyone is a gold expert
- Relatives who never discussed investments are now recommending "buy gold, beta"
- Jewellers are packed
- Gold ETF inflows hit all-time highs
- Every second YouTube video has "GOLD" in capital letters

Then a correction happens (as it always does at some point):

- The same people who were confidently buying at the top are now "waiting for it to stabilize"
- Some panic-sell
- Regret cycle begins

**Indians treat gold like IPL auctions.**

Everyone wants the player after his best season.

![Investor Psychology Cycle](/assets/images/posts/gold-psychology.png)

This is not unique to Indians. This is universal human psychology. We are wired to chase momentum and avoid things that feel "boring." Gold during a flat phase feels boring. Gold during a crisis rally feels exciting.

But investing and excitement are usually opposites.

The boring entry. The patient hold. The boring exit.

That's what actually builds wealth.

Let's look at the real numbers. Not cherry-picked returns. Annualised returns across multiple timeframes — compared to what else your money could have done.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gold (INR) | ~25–30% | ~18% | ~16% | ~12% | ~13% |
| Nifty 50 (Index) | ~12–15% | ~14% | ~14% | ~13% | ~14% |
| Fixed Deposits | ~7% | ~7% | ~7% | ~7% | ~7% |
| India Inflation | ~5–6% | ~5% | ~5% | ~5% | ~6% |

*Note: Returns are approximate historical figures and not guaranteed. Past returns do not predict future performance.*

A few things to notice:

**Over 1–3 years:** Gold looks incredible right now — because of the current crisis-driven rally.

**Over 10–20 years:** Gold and Nifty 50 are surprisingly close in annualised returns, roughly 12–14%.

**Over any timeframe:** Gold comfortably beats inflation and fixed deposits.

What does this tell us?

Gold is an excellent **wealth preserver**.

It protects your purchasing power over long periods. It hedges against currency devaluation. It tends to rise when equity markets fall.

But gold is not a consistent **wealth creator** in the way that equity compounding is. You don't get dividends. You don't get earnings growth. You get a metal that people trust during uncertainty.

The moment you understand this distinction, gold becomes a tool in your portfolio — not a panic purchase.

## Should You Stop Buying Gold Completely?

No.

That's not the point of this article.

Gold has real, legitimate roles in an Indian household and portfolio:

**Cultural significance:** Weddings, festivals, gifting traditions — gold is deeply embedded in Indian life. That's not going away and it shouldn't.

**Portfolio diversification:** Gold often moves opposite to equities. When stock markets crash, gold tends to hold or rise. This reduces overall portfolio volatility.

**Hedge against rupee depreciation:** India has historically seen the rupee weaken over long periods. Gold, being dollar-priced, provides a natural hedge against this.

**Psychological stability:** During extreme uncertainty, holding some gold gives people peace of mind. That has value.

**No counterparty risk:** Unlike bank FDs, company bonds, or stocks — physical gold doesn't have a counterparty. Nobody can go bankrupt and take your gold away.

All of this is real. All of this matters.

The problem isn't gold.

The problem is **FOMO buying at peak prices** — which is what happens to most retail investors.

- Buying a large lump sum after a 50% rally: risky
- Buying because "everyone is buying": dangerous
- Treating gold as a short-term trade: expensive
- Over-allocating to gold at the cost of equity growth: costly mistake over 20+ years

That's what this article is warning against.

## A Smarter Strategy for Gold

Here's how to actually use gold well:

**1. Keep allocation between 5–10% of your portfolio**

Gold should be a supporting role in your portfolio, not the lead. Most financial planners recommend 5–10%. If your total investable portfolio is ₹20 lakh, that means ₹1–2 lakh in gold exposure — not ₹10 lakh.

**2. Use Gold ETFs or Gold Mutual Funds for investment purposes**

Physical gold has making charges, storage costs, safety risks, and purity concerns. Gold ETFs give you the same price exposure without the headaches. You can also do a **monthly SIP** into a Gold ETF — which automatically averages your purchase price over time.

**3. Don't time the market with lump sums after rallies**

If you're entering gold now after a 60% rally, at least stagger your purchases over 6–12 months instead of going all-in today.

**4. Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) when available**

SGBs (when the government issues them) are excellent — you get gold price returns plus 2.5% annual interest, and long-term capital gains tax is waived if held to maturity. Watch for new issuances.

**5. Prioritise emergency fund and equity first**

Before thinking about gold as an investment, make sure your emergency fund is sorted (6 months of expenses in liquid instruments) and your equity SIPs are running. Gold comes after the basics.

**6. Treat physical gold purchases (jewellery) separately**

Jewellery bought for weddings or cultural reasons isn't really an "investment." Don't confuse the two. Buy what you need for the occasion. Don't justify it as a portfolio decision.

## The Real Lesson From PM Modi's Statement

PM Modi's request wasn't just patriotic messaging.

It was also — accidentally — very good personal finance advice.

He was saying: during a global crisis, when dollar demand is high and the rupee is weakening, **this is the most expensive time for Indians to buy gold.** You pay more for the dollar. You pay more for the gold. And you're doing it at a moment of peak global fear — which is historically not the best time for any investment decision.

Think about the full chain of events:

1. Global crisis erupts
2. Investors worldwide panic and buy gold → gold price rises in dollars
3. Simultaneously, emerging market currencies weaken → dollar strengthens
4. Indian rupee falls → gold becomes even more expensive in rupees
5. Indian retail investors, seeing gold at all-time highs on TV, rush to buy
6. India's forex burden increases → puts pressure on the economy
7. The investor bought at peak prices AND contributed to macro stress

Everyone loses except the person who bought gold quietly 18 months ago.

Indians don't buy gold when it is cheap.

We wait for gold to hit all-time highs and suddenly call it safe.

Buy high, panic during corrections, repeat again next wedding season.

Gold is not the problem. The timing is.

The best time people wanted gold was yesterday.

The second best time is after the panic disappears — not during it.

**Gold is meant to protect wealth. Not become your panic purchase during every global crisis.**

So the next time you see a "GOLD AT ALL TIME HIGH 🚨" headline — take a breath.

One of those is investing.

The other one is just expensive FOMO with a gold sticker on it.
